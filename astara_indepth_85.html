01Overview

The Long Haul
Goes Electric

In 2026, doubting electric mobility in passenger vehicles requires willful ignorance. The numbers are too clear. But heavy trucks? Until recently, they were the last holdout.

2025 changed that. Electric truck sales doubled globally, from 220,000 to 440,000 units. Market share climbed from 4.5% to 9%: a milestone that puts heavy transport on the same trajectory passenger EVs were on in 2019–2020.

97% of those sales are battery electric. The industry isn't hedging on hydrogen or range extenders: it's betting on pure BEV. But two structural challenges remain that passenger EVs never faced at this scale.

Challenge 1: Range
Passenger EVs now routinely exceed 700km. Trucks carry larger packs but also heavier loads over longer distances. Today's long-haul EV trucks are closing the gap fast (the best models already reach 700-800km), but the infrastructure to exploit that range in commercial operations isn't yet there.
Challenge 2: Charging Infrastructure
A medium truck on urban routes returns to a private depot every night. A long-haul heavy truck covering 1,000km has nowhere to charge fast enough. Battery packs on heavy trucks require ultra-rapid charging (400kW+); standard public EV chargers are irrelevant.

"The commercial freight industry operates on razor-thin margins. For fleet operators, switching to EV trucks is a purely mathematical decision. The math is starting to work."

The gap has been growing consistently since 2020. China was at 15,000 units that year, already ahead. By 2025, it was at 405,000. Europe went from near-zero to 16,800, a real gain, but still 24× smaller than China's market. North America is barely on the chart.

Why China pulled so far ahead
Three forces combined: (1) urban delivery zones that banned diesel, forcing fleet electrification; (2) state-supported private depot charging and a network of commercial battery swap stations, solving the charging problem without waiting for public infrastructure; (3) an intensely competitive domestic manufacturing base (BYD, SANY, Sinotruk, FAW) that compressed prices faster than anywhere else.

Notice the natural gas peak: NG surged from 25% to 30% share in 2024, then retreated to 25% in 2025 as BEV improved. This is the classic "bridge technology" pattern: a cleaner interim solution crests just as the clean alternative reaches cost parity.

Why EREVs were skipped entirely
Extended-Range Electric Vehicles (battery EV with a backup fuel generator) seem logical given the charging gap. The market bypassed them. Reason: EREVs add mechanical complexity that reduces payload capacity and increases maintenance costs. Battery swap stations solve the charging problem more elegantly. The industry concluded EREVs offer fleet operators the worst of both worlds.

The critical read: BEV trucks save massively on fuel ($0.13 vs $0.39 for diesel) and modestly on maintenance. But the vehicle acquisition cost is 3x higher: $0.19/km amortized versus $0.06 for diesel. Natural gas trucks currently win on total cost, which explains why NG still holds 25% share. As battery prices continue falling, BEV will cross below $0.79/km, likely within 1-2 years for the Chinese market.

"BEV trucks already beat diesel on TCO in China. They haven't yet beaten natural gas. That gap closes as battery packs get cheaper. And the regulations don't wait."

What the Numbers
Mean for What's Next

The electric truck transition is real, accelerating, and deeply concentrated. Here's what fleet operators, OEMs, and infrastructure investors need to take from the 2025 data:

  • The China playbook is the template. Battery swap networks + aggressive manufacturing pricing + regulatory pressure = mass adoption. Europe and North America are running the same play 3-5 years behind.
  • Short-haul is solved. Long-haul is the bottleneck. Urban delivery trucks are already cost-competitive on TCO. Long-haul heavy trucks still face the ultra-fast highway charging gap: that's the next critical infrastructure build-out, and the next investment opportunity.
  • Battery cost is the remaining pivot point. When packs reach $80/kWh, the TCO math flips conclusively in favor of BEV across all segments. At current trajectory, that's 2027–2028 for most markets.
  • Natural gas peaked. NG trucks hit 30% in China in 2024, then retreated to 25% in 2025 as BEV economics improved. In Europe, regulators are skipping the bridge altogether. Natural gas is a transition, not a destination.
  • Watch SANY, FAW, and BYD in Europe. Chinese manufacturers now field world-class products with significant cost advantages. The pattern that played out in passenger EVs is beginning in trucks. Western incumbents have a shrinking window.
